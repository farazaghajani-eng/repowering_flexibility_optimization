# METHODOLOGY

## Research Framework
This study employs a systematic research framework integrating theoretical and practical aspects of power system optimization. The framework encompasses literature review, model development, and validation through case studies.

## Optimization Approach
We utilize a multi-objective optimization approach combining cost minimization and performance maximization. The framework facilitates stakeholder engagement and feedback in refining optimization processes.

## Mathematical Formulation
The optimization problem is defined mathematically as:  

**Objective Function:**  
Minimize: \[ C(x) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} c_i \cdot x_i \]  
Where, $x_i$ represents decision variables involved in system repowering.

**Constraints:**  
1. \[ g_j(x) \leq 0 \] (Equality constraints)  
2. \[ h_k(x) = 0 \] (Inequality constraints)

## Case Studies
We present five case studies illustrating the application of the optimization framework in real-world scenarios:
- Case Study 1: Urban Electricity Grid
- Case Study 2: Rural Electrification Projects
- Case Study 3: Hybrid Energy Systems
- Case Study 4: Wind and Solar Integration
- Case Study 5: Policy Impact Analysis

## Data Sources
Data were sourced from:
1. National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL)
2. International Energy Agency (IEA)
3. Local Utility Providers

## Solver Methodology
The optimization problem is solved using advanced algorithms, including:
- Genetic Algorithms (GA)
- Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO)
- Linear Programming (LP)

## Performance Metrics
The following metrics are utilized for performance evaluation:
- Cost Efficiency
- Renewable Integration Ratio
- System Reliability

## Sensitivity Analysis
Sensitivity analysis is conducted to evaluate the robustness of the optimization model against dataset anomalies and variations in input parameters. Key findings include:
- Effect of renewable energy variations on system performance.
- Cost sensitivity concerning financial variables.

## Limitations
The study acknowledges several limitations:
1. Data Availability: Incomplete datasets can lead to optimization errors.
2. Model Simplifications: Assumptions made for model tractability may oversimplify real-world dynamics.
3. Computational Complexity: The complexity of algorithms increases with larger datasets.
